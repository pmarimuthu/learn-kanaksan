# Chapter 3 — Motion in a Plane

<ConceptMeta chapter="3" section="3.1–3.8" page="38–61" />

This chapter extends the language of motion from a straight line to **two dimensions**. The new tool you need is the **vector** — a quantity with both size and direction. With vectors you can describe a cricket ball's flight path, a river boat's drift, or a satellite's orbit.

Work through the concepts in order. Each page follows the same plan: the idea in plain words, the formula with symbols named, a real-world example, a home experiment, a "what if" to think about, and a quick check.

## Concepts in this chapter

1. [Scalars and Vectors](./scalars-and-vectors) — what a direction does to a number.
2. [Vector Addition](./vector-addition) — triangle law, parallelogram law, and when you subtract.
3. [Components of a Vector](./vector-components) — breaking any vector into its x and y parts.
4. [Unit Vectors](./unit-vectors) — the direction-only arrows î, ĵ, k̂.
5. [Relative Velocity in 2D](./relative-velocity-2d) — the boat-in-river problem.
6. [Projectile Motion](./projectile-motion) — why a thrown ball follows a parabola.
7. [Uniform Circular Motion](./uniform-circular-motion) — constant speed, changing direction, centripetal acceleration.

## The one-line summary

> Motion in a plane is described by vectors — quantities with magnitude and direction — and every 2D problem can be split into two independent 1D problems along x and y.
